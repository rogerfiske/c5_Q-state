Introduction 
About 
This paper proposes Transformable Patching Graph Neural 
Networks (T-PATCHGNN), a novel framework for Irregular 
Multivariate Time Series (IMTS) forecasting that avoids canonical pre-
alignment while effectively capturing both intra-time series 
dependencies and inter-time series correlations. The model 
integrates Transformer-based sequential modeling and time-
adaptive graph neural networks to capture dynamic inter-

--- Page 2 ---
variable relationships. Extensive experiments on diverse real-world 
datasets demonstrate that T-PATCHGNN achieves state-of-the-art 
forecasting accuracy, superior scalability, and significantly 
improved efficiency compared to existing approaches. 
Background

An irregular multivariate time series (IMTS) consists of 
multiple time-dependent variables, each observed at non-uniform 
and asynchronous timestamps. Formally, different variables may 

--- Page 3 ---
have distinct observation times and frequencies, leading to severe 
misalignment across dimensions. As illustrated in Figure 1 of the 
paper, even when variables cover the same overall time span, their 
observations may be sparse, unevenly distributed, 
and temporally inconsistent. This makes it difficult to directly 
apply standard multivariate forecasting models, which typically 
assume synchronized inputs. 
A common approach to handling IMTS is to convert them 
into canonical pre-aligned representations by enumerating all 
unique timestamps across variables and filling missing values with 
masks. Although this representation simplifies downstream modeling, 
it introduces a critical drawback: the resulting sequence length 
grows rapidly with the number of variables, causing 
a sequence length explosion that severely limits scalability. 
Alternative continuous-time models, such as neural ODE–based 
methods, address irregularity by modeling latent state evolution 
between observations, but they often incur high computational 
cost and focus primarily on intra-variable dynamics. These limitations 
motivate the need for a representation that can simultaneously 
handle irregularity, asynchrony, and inter-variable 
dependencies without sacrificing efficiency.

Irregular Time Series Patching 
Transformable Patching 
The core idea of transformable patching is to divide each univariate 
irregular time series into a sequence of patches with variable 
numbers of observations but fixed temporal span. Unlike 
standard patching, which groups a fixed number of points, this method 
ensures that each patch corresponds to a consistent time horizon (e.g., 
2 hours), thereby normalizing temporal resolution across 
variables. This design resolves the inconsistency caused by 
irregular sampling, where equal-sized observation windows 
may span drastically different time intervals. 
Importantly, patching is performed independently for each 
variable, avoiding the need for global time alignment. As a result, the 

--- Page 7 ---
sequence length depends only on the number of patches per 
variable rather than the total number of unique timestamps 
across variables. This directly mitigates the sequence length 
explosion problem described in the preliminary section. Moreover, the 
patch-level representation provides richer local context, 
which improves the model’s ability to capture short-term temporal 
dynamics.

Experiment 
The experiment in this literature aimed to comprehensively evaluate 
the effectiveness of T-PATCHGNN on irregular multivariate time 
series forecasting tasks. The authors constructed a diverse 
benchmark covering multiple real-world domains and compared 
against a wide range of baselines spanning forecasting, classification, 
and interpolation tasks. Through these experiments, they assess not 
only predictive performance but also scalability and robustness of the 
proposed method.

Overall, the ablation study confirms that each component 
contributes meaningfully to the overall performance, with 
transformable patching and graph-based correlation 
modeling being the most impactful.

This experiment analyzes the impact of patch window size on 
forecasting performance, as illustrated in Figure 4. The results show 
that optimal patch size is dataset-dependent. 
For PhysioNet and MIMIC, performance is stable for small patches 
and peaks around 8 hours, balancing sufficient observations within 
each patch and temporal resolution. Too small patches lack 
informative context, while overly large patches degrade resolution. 
In contrast, Human Activity and USHCN benefit from smaller 
patch sizes, as their more dynamic patterns require finer temporal 
granularity. The authors further note that larger patches favor 
long-range forecasting by capturing broader trends, 
whereas smaller patches are better for short-range 
predictions. This highlights patch size as a key factor 
controlling the trade-off between temporal resolution and 
contextual richness. 
Conclusion 
This paper introduces T-PATCHGNN, a novel framework for 
irregular multivariate time series forecasting that effectively addresses 
the challenges of irregular sampling, asynchrony, and scalability. By 
proposing transformable patching, the method avoids the 
limitations of canonical pre-alignment while preserving local 
temporal semantics. The integration of Transformer-based intra-
series modeling and time-adaptive graph neural networks enables 
comprehensive modeling of both temporal dependencies and dynamic 

--- Page 21 ---
inter-variable correlations. Extensive experiments on diverse real-
world datasets demonstrate consistent performance 
improvements over a wide range of baselines. 
Review 
This paper presents a thoughtfully designed framework that tackles 
three fundamental challenges in IMTS 
forecasting: irregularity, asynchrony, and scalability. The key 
strength lies in the transformable patching mechanism, which 
elegantly replaces the widely used pre-alignment strategy and 
directly addresses the sequence length explosion issue. By 
combining this representation with Transformer-based temporal 
modeling and adaptive graph learning, the model captures both 
intra- and inter-series dependencies in a unified manner. The 
experimental evaluation is comprehensive, covering multiple domains 
and a wide range of baselines, and the consistent performance gains 
strongly support the effectiveness of the proposed approach. The 
inclusion of scalability analysis further strengthens the 
practical relevance of the work. 
However, the framework also introduces a relatively complex 
architecture that integrates multiple components, which 
may increase implementation difficulty and hyperparameter 
sensitivity. While the model demonstrates strong empirical 
performance, its reliance on carefully designed patch sizes and 
graph learning mechanisms may limit its robustness in highly 

--- Page 22 ---
heterogeneous settings. Also, although the method avoids ODE-based 
computation, the combination of Transformer and GNN 
modules could still pose challenges for deployment in 
resource-constrained environments. Future work could explore 
simplifying the architecture, automating patch construction, or 
incorporating more lightweight modeling techniques. Despite these 
considerations, the paper makes a significant contribution to 
IMTS forecasting and provides a promising direction for 
future research in handling irregular and asynchronous time 
series data.
